## Purpose of this reference article

Before jumping into the Projects and Routines collections, it's important to understand the distinction between the two and the order of operations from a time management standpoint.

**Shifting your mind to view everything as a project or routine will ultimately help you better manage your time, focus, and energy.**

Wrting down your projects and routines gives you an inventory to work from (for proactive selection, prioritization, improvements, mapping out details). This is where the intangible becomes tangible and where you can interact with that inventory in a way you could not if it solely lived in your mind.

Routines and projects should be in line with your core principles, priorties, and identity – projects and routines should support all three. Conversely, the latter should be used to help you prioritize your projects and routines. This is where the components of the extended mind system beging to interact and influence one another.

*Some of us view these things intuitively and follow this break down, but I'm not sure anyone has ever really put it into words, at least no in this manner. Most people do all of this on some level but they are scratching the surface. By writing it down, they can go deeper and be more effective in the choices they make and how they spend their time and energy.*

## So why on earth does this distinction matter?

1. Both projects and routines consume resources: time, energy, and money. So to manage your projects and routines well is to manage your resources well.
2. The distinction impacts the order of operations in terms of what you prioritize to work on next.


### An Accounting System for Time and Energy

At the core of all of this and thinking in this way, is ensuring that you are making the most of the resources available to you (time, energy, and money).

Time and energy need to be budgeted in the same way money is.

Routines and projects become the line items in your time and energy budget.

To find savings in your time and energy, you must review the individual line items and either cut them or reduce them. Or! say, I am okay with spending X amount of time and energy here and this is why.

And rather than try to do this all in your head (and risk distorting reality) we write these things down to externalize them (that is we make the intagible, tangible) so that we can examine them and decide.

Decide what to keep, what to get rid of, what to add more of, and what to improve.

You would never try to do math in your head when it comes to financial budgeting. Well guess what? The hours you have in a week or month or year is math. How those minutes and hours get divied up is math. That invidisble meter you have that represent the cognitive energy and will power you have each day, is math. 

So we externalize these elements so we can get an actualy ACCOUNT of where were are allocating our time, energy, and money.

And just like with financial budgets, when we write it out, we often start wondering or are amazed with where those dollars go.

Also, by writing it down, you may realize you are trying to do way too much or spending your time on things that you don't really want your time going to.

⭐️ ***To optimize routines is to optimize your time and energy.*** ⭐️

*Caveat: Optimization isn't just about neccessity and efficiency. It's about ensuring those simple moments in life – grabbing a cup of coffee, ironing clothes, planning out the week, sitting down to dinner each night with your family – are also pleasant. Because you should enjoy those little moments just as much as you do the novel moments and milestones. The in-between, the day-to-day, is just as important.*

In the context of time management, it's not just about managing your time; it's about managing your energy. Sure, you might technically have the hours, but if your brain is fried from being at work all day, or your brain is fried because you just spent an hour in traffic and it's frazzled your nerves, or you're exhausted from sitting on Zoom calls all day, you may have the time, but it doesn't mean that you have the energy. That's something that you have to keep in mind as you're mapping out your routines and your projects and figuring out what you want to focus on. You have to make sure that you have both in place.

You might have an aspiration to work a full-time job while also writing a book, or you may have an aspiration to run a marathon while also working a full-time job. You have to be cognizant about when you pursue those projects and when you pursue those routines, making sure that both elements are in place: that you have the time and you have the energy. A lot of people have good intentions. They get home at night and they're just wiped out from the day, and then they mentally beat themselves up because they feel like they should be doing more. They just don't have the energy, and so people talk about time, time, time, but nobody talks about energy.

I don't want you beating yourself up because you're not getting to something, because you simply just don't have the energy. If that becomes a blocker for you, you need to figure out, in the way that we say we need to make time for things, we also have to make sure that we have the energy available. It may not be a time issue for you. It may be, "I need to reassess where my energy is going and how can I conserve some of that energy so that I have it for the things that I want to do." And that's where you start to get into a conversation about your environment, the variables: what is taking up that energy? Are you doing things that are draining you? Are you doing things that are energizing you? Are you losing energy to inefficient routines or a million decisions? You have to assess that it's not just time, but where is that energy going now? Where your energy is going is again one of those things that starts to get into the intangible.

Once again, writing things down, you start to realize all the things that are pulling on your attention, all the things that are pulling on your energy, in addition to where your time is going. That's where you can start making better decisions about: am I happy with the status quo? Am I happy with where things are set up? Is this how I want to allocate my days? That's where you can start making informed decisions. You can start using not what you imagine in your mind to be, but what actually is, to make decisions about how you want to move forward.

#### What the routines collection does for you.

To begin, daily routines do not get written down in the routines collection. That is not to say that you shouldn't write them down. It is worth keeping a page or two where you say, "This is what my weekday looks like, this is what a weekend looks like". It is important to write it out because writing out your daily routines and what a typical day looks like for you is important, because that is going to be how you create the map, the inventory, and how you can assess. Granted, a weekday may look very different from a weekend, but writing out what that typical day looks like for you is important because that is going to be how you create the map, the inventory, and how you can assess.
It is a good exercise to go through and thoroughly document all your daily routines, from:
- sleep
- skincare
- cooking
- daily tidying
- social activities
- scrolling social media
Now, the reason why we do not put daily routines in the routines collection is because you would end up with 365 instances of every one of those routines. It begins to clutter the database. Also, your daily routines really should be automatic. I say routines are the automations of life. That is especially true for daily routines. If you want to optimize your daily routines, they should be so ingrained, so second nature, the systems in place that you can go about them without thinking about it.
- You shouldn't think about brushing your teeth.
- You shouldn't think about making your bed in the morning.
- You shouldn't think about getting dressed.
- You shouldn't think about making yourself coffee.
Like, all of those are little micro routines and habits that exist throughout the day, and everything should be set up to support those. We aren't going to write those down in the collection.

Now, what we do write down in the routines collection is non-daily routines. Anything that's happening every week, bi-weekly, monthly, annually, et cetera. The reason why we write those down is because:
1. It helps us have a list. When a new month rolls over, a new quarter rolls over, a new season starts, we know, "Okay, for whatever given time period, I know that these are the things that I'm going to want and need to do within this block of time, whatever that block is that you set."
2. Because these aren't daily routines, we aren't doing them frequently enough to necessarily commit them to memory.
Let me go back to my recipe example here: If you're cooking a recipe every single day, like if you make scrambled eggs every day at lunch, you know how to make scrambled eggs. You have everything you need right there: you have the steps, you have the process, and you just execute, right? That's what we want for a daily routine: we want something that has everything there, and you really don't have to think about it. You just execute it.
Say you make a dish every year at Thanksgiving, and you are only like, every year you make it, but you only do it one time a year. You're not going to commit that to memory. We write it down so we know, "Hey, this routine's coming up when the fall rolls around. I know I'm going to need to get certain things in place so I can make the routine." You have the steps written down; you have the ingredients; you know the equipment that you use, so you have the whole recipe written down so you don't have to retain it in your memory. You don't have to re-invent the wheel, because if you didn't have it written down, what you're going to do is, every year when Thanksgiving rolls around, you're going to be like, "Okay, what are the routines that I need to do again? What are the steps to those routines?" When you start relying on your memory in that situation, that's when you're going to forget things. The day is going to come. You're going to go cook, and you're going to realize you forgot an ingredient or the timing is off. By writing it out, you don't reinvent the wheel. You know everything that you're going to need for that routine, and you're going to know all the time frames for that routine.
Same thing if you do something on a weekly basis. Say once a week you want to get to an inbox zero routine, and you have a checklist for everything you need to check, everything that you need to review. You're not using your mind to remember what it is you need to check. You're using your mind to actually do the thing. You're relying on the written down routine to tell you, "Here is the checklist of everywhere you need to go and look." You don't have to remember that checklist. You externalize that checklist. You reference that checklist. You pull that checklist up when you need it and you reference it, but you just focus on going through the actions.
Writing down routines allows you to execute quicker because you're not having to remember all the details around that routine that you are not doing on a daily basis. Writing routines down does a couple of things for you.

Writing routines down allows you to get an accurate inventory of everything you actually want and need to do. These become the line items in your budget, so you can actually look at those line items and decide: is this something that I want, need? Should I keep it, or do I need to modify this in some way or delete it entirely?

Writing it down also lets you identify gaps. Maybe you realize aspirationally there is a routine that you want to do, but it has not actually been incorporated. That is a gap where you can say, OK, I need to go about a project to establish this routine or improve this routine so that it actually becomes a routine item to me to maintain whatever baseline I'm trying to maintain.  

Writing routines allows you to externalize:
- the routine
- the steps
- the ingredients
- the equipment
- the tools
- the timelines
- the contact information
- the locations
Any piece of information that you require to execute that routine, any item you need, any input, anything that you need to do from a person, place, or thing, or piece of information to execute that routine. That routine, to the steps, to the checklist, to instructions, whatever you need to successfully implement that routine, is all contained on a page. You don't have to hold that in your mind. You can write it down once, and you can forget about it until it's time to do that routine. Then you can pull up your page and say, "OK, this is what I need to do."
The other thing is that when you start writing down routines, just like I said, writing down daily routines is worthwhile. Writing down any routine is worthwhile because when you actually write out all of the details and you put it on a piece of paper, this is something that I picked up from the business world and documenting business processes. When you start writing things down and actually mapping them out, that is where you can start saying, "Oh, that step's unnecessary," or "Oh, these two or three things could be combined," or "Oh, here's something I could do to help improve." Before you can really improve anything and make it better, it helps to have it written down again, because we aren't good about working through those tasks and those items in our mind necessarily. Some people are; most of us aren't. It helps for us to write it down so that we have something visually to work with and manipulate again, to make the intangible tangible.

And by writing it down, we start creating records. When the month or the quarter rolls around, you pull up your list and you're like, "Okay, here's everything that I need to do." Again, going back to the budgeting example, it's not just about what you do on a daily basis. You have so many hours in a month, so many hours in a season. If you come upon fall and you've got a bunch of routines that you do in fall, like that, coupled with your daily routines, your weekly routines, that all collectively goes into that life accounting system. ## Why I do not believe in Task or TODO Lists.

I haven't used a task or to do list in years. Everything I do exists

A task list, psychologically, does not capture the full scope of what needs to be done. A task sounds simple. A one off. A one and done. But rarely does an isolated task exist on it's own accord.

Even think of something as simple as a phone call to customer service. There is usually tasks the preceed before the call or have to be completed after the call.

Here is a simple example. You notice an erranous charge on your credit card. So you put something on your TODO list to follow up with customer service. But that 'task' is not a single task. Not really. Because what do you do in this situation? You notice the charge. You probably login to your account or check your email because you thought you had cancelled the subscription or you didn't get an alert on a charge. Now you have to look up the contact methods available. Pick a method. Then contact. Either have the phone call or email exchange. Resolve the issue (which may take more than one call). Then remember to check your card to ensure the refund was compelted. And double check the subscription to make sure you won't be charged again.

Or, say you want to paint a room. Or prep for Thanksgiving dinner (something you do every year – a routine). People wil write this on their 'to do' list and then hold all the tasks associated with line item in their head. Which is what causes distraction and open loops.

So a TODO list if a step in the right direction in terms of offloading the item from your mind so you don't forget and can focus. However, it's an incomplete approach. What you need is to write the things down then write down all the tasks associated with it. So, as a write this, a TODO list can exist with the context of a project or a routine. But a task list cannot exisit outside these objects.

Most people's TODO list is really a collection of projects and routines. And this is something I learned from David Allen's Getting Things Done. I read it. It was an AHA moment for me and really shifted how I go about logging what I want and need to do and how I select / priortize amongst those lists.

And this is one place where writing things down really helps you focus. I talk about this in why keep an extended mind system, but I will reiterate some of the points here.
- By writing down a project (to include tasks, reference materials, notes, etc.) you are releasing that thought from your brain so you don't lose and can focus.
- Not every project is going to get done. This is especially true for those of us who are idea generation machines. We're going to have far more ideas than it possible to execute on or execute well on. So we have to choose. But just because we don't have the resources to allocate to every project, doesn't mean our brain isn't going to stop coming up with the idea. Our brains don't care if we don't have the time, energy, or money to pursue a thing. It's going to think of it anyways. And it's going to keep thinking of it until we externalize it. At that point, you can say, I have the idea, I've written down, I won't lose it, and it's now there and waiting for if and when I choose to pursue it.
- The act of creating a container gives you a place to collect threads. Writing down the idea doesn't stop you from ever thinking about it again. Now that you've written down the idea, your brain may go a bit deeper and say, okay, you've written it down, now here's a task or sub-project to support it. So now you have to write that next level down to stop yourself from forgetting that next layer. Or, you may come across a piece of information, a product, an article, etc. that could be helpful if and when you ever decide to tackle that project. So having a page for the project gives you a place to capture that note. Again, which releases it from your mind so you can go back to focusing on whatever it is you were doing.
