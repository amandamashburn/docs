## A routine to audit your routines.

If you have never thought much about your routines or you have gone through a major life change, a routine audit is a great way to map out your days and your weeks so you know where your time is being allocated and if changes or improvement needs to be made.

### Questions a routine audit answers
A routine audit is an excellent exercise to assess
- Where your time is actually going.
- Why do you even have said routines? Where did they come from? Why do you keep them? Why are they constructed how they are?
- Are there routines that need to be dropped, added, or modified.
- Can routines be merged and combined?
- Does your reality match your aspirations? Do your routines reflect your idenitiy, core princriples, values, and priorities?
- Are there gaps in my routines? Are there areas that are being neglected? Consider the whole person, family, community, etc. here.

In essence, you need an ad hoc routine for auditing your routines. An ad hoc routine that is triggered by a change in circumstances.

#### Options for streamlining routines.

There is nothing so useless as doing efficiently that which should not be done at all. – Peter Drucker

The very first thing you are going to ask yourself is: what can I delete? This can apply to en entire routine, a sub-routine, or tasks within a routine.

Delete as much as you can before you do anything else.

- Drop tasks and sub-routines.
- Make QoL improvements: a new bed to improve sleep, new pans to simplify cooking eggs each day, switching to public transportation to avoid the hassle of parking, making coffee at home to avoid an extra stop in your morning commute, buy an air compressor so you can air up your tires at home.

- Build systems and zones in your home: Keep all your gardening tools together so you don't have to pull them from 5 different places.
- Merge and combine routines (habit stacking): This is a great way to minimize overhead costs of a routine.
- Reduce frequency to minimize overhead costs.
- Switch to batch processing.

### Updating routines (the first year).

So something I want you to keep in mind when you are first starting to write down your routines is that you're probably going to miss things. You're going to miss routines, you're going to miss steps.

I would say your routines collection: give it an actual year before you feel comfortable with the state of it. I say that because as you go through the year, you're going to naturally find that you do things. I want you to pause, whether it's a new month or a new season, or even a weekly routine. You're going to actually observe yourself and what routines you're actually doing, what they look like, when they occur, how frequently they occur, and what they trigger. Because a lot of us have an annual routine, for example, an annual doctor's appointment or once a year we file our taxes, you want to give yourself a full year to be able to really observe yourself and capture what you're really doing. Then, of course, to add notes throughout, just create the collection and add to it as you go. This is one that you really want to let emerge naturally. Don't sit down and try to get it perfect from day one. You're not going to.

Create the collection, create the frame rate, create the structure so you have a place to store this information, and then, as you observe yourself conducting a routine, you observe the frequency, you observe the steps, just note as you go. Now, you, of course, can do an initial pass if you want, but I really would give yourself a full year for complete seasons. It doesn't need to start on January 1st. Just start today and watch yourself over the next year and see what you're actually repeating and how often you're doing it and what those routines look like. When you get 12 months down the road, you're actually going to have a better idea of the full scope of your routines throughout the year. That will help you when you get 12 months down the road, if you can consistently use this collection. That will help you when the next year rolls around. You kind of know what's going to be on the radar for that year, and it's going to help you plan your time better.

Just a little side note: I've seen farmers do this, and I love this idea. They'll say, "I've got 40 plantings left in my life," or "I've got 32 Christmases left," or "70 birthdays left." I think that's a very interesting frame of mind to look at things, because it really does help us recognize that our time is unlimited. How many birthdays do you have left? How many holidays do you have left with your family? If you are a gardener, how many more plantings do you get to do?
