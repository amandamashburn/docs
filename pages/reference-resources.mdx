Every decision and action costs something.

Every decision you make allocates resources – time, energy, and money. Every action you take spends some of them. Because they are finite, they are as much a constraint as they are a resource. How you spend them, in aggregate, is how you spend your life.

Most have internalized the idea of budgeting money. Many have started applying the same thinking to time. Fewer have extended that discipline to include energy – mental and physical – as well. Even fewer are considering all three factors in every decision they make.

The goal here is to get these resources on your radar so you begin to consistently account for them. Once you do, decision-making improves. You stop treating time like it's free, attention like it's infinite, and money like it's inconsequential.

With each decision, you begin to ask:
- What is this going to cost me in terms of time, energy, and money?
- Is this a good use of my resources?
- Is this where I want to allocate them?

And more broadly:
- Am I letting the resources available to me go to waste – through underuse or misallocation?
- If yes, how do I course correct?

*Note that just because something is a good use of resources does not necessarily mean you need to allocate them there.*

## The three core constraints

### Time

Time is your most valuable resource, and it is the only one that cannot be replenished, earned back, or accumulated. An hour spent is gone. There is no refund. You also don't know exactly how much of it you have – you may be granted 18 years or 100.

Most already know this in the abstract. But, few actually budget their time with discipline. The default is drift: saying yes to things that don't matter, letting low-value activities fill high-value hours, and arriving at the end of the week with a vague sense of having been busy but not necessarily spending the time well (i.e., on your priorities).

**What wastes it:** Automatically saying yes to everything. Constant context-switching, decision-making, and re-work. High friction environments. Reacting. Lack of planning.

**How to get more from it:** Do less, better. Be selective – not passive – about where your time goes. Focus on a few areas instead of spreading yourself thin. Build routines that eliminate low-stakes and repititive decisions. Be clear about your priorities and values.

### Energy

Energy is a constraint that comes in two forms – cognitive and physical. Most treat them as separate and unrelated. They are not. They are two sides of the same coin, each influencing the other. Constantly fighting your enviornment (mentally) can sap your motivation to move. Physical neglect degrades focus and willpower.

Both forms of energy share one defining characteristic: they do not accumulate. Whatever you don't use today does not carry over. It's a use it or lose it situation.

#### Cognitive energy

Cognitive energy is the mental fuel that powers all effortful thought – attention, reasoning, decision-making, learning, and self-control. It is finite. It degrades with use. And it does not accumulate: whatever cognitive energy you don't use today doesn't carry over. If you coast through Tuesday, you don't show up Wednesday with a surplus.

The brain defaults toward low-effort processing – habit, pattern recognition, intuition – partly as conservation. When you override those defaults with deliberate thought, the resource drains faster. This is why hard decisions feel cumbersome at the end of a long day. It's why a dense chapter of a difficult book can leave you more tired than a long walk.

**Attention is a useful sub-concept here.** If cognitive energy is how much mental fuel you have, attention is where you direct it. You can have a full tank and still waste it by spreading yourself too thin. Or waste it by spending it on activities that are the equivalent of junk food for the mind.

**What wastes it:** Decision fatigue – too many small decisions accumulating before you get to the important ones. A chaotic or friction-heavy environment that demands constant problem-solving. Poor sleep, dehydration, or skipped meals. Passive distraction disguised as rest (endless scrolling is not recovery).

**How to get more from it:** Protect your high-energy hours for your highest priorites. Design your environment to reduce friction rather than constantly fighting it. Build routines that put low-stakes decisions on autopilot. Prioritize sleep, movement, and the basics – these are not optional inputs.

#### Physical energy

It's time to return to something that was understood clearly in antiquity: the body and mind are not separate. Physical discipline supports mental clarity. Physical neglect undermines it.

Physical energy is the body's output capacity. For some – athletes, tradesmen, military – this constraint is considered daily. They are pushing against it and managing it accordingly. For the rest of us, it tends to go underutilized. And therin lies the issue.

Of the core constraints, physical energy is the one most consistently left on the table. Most people have never come close to their physical limits and are wasting what they have been given. The body needs to be physically drained regularly to be at your best in all aspects of life.

If you have a physically demanding lifestyle – manage your output.

For those with a less demanding lifestlye – work towards maximizing this resource.

**What wastes it:** Chronic sedentary behavior. Neglecting recovery (sleep, nutrition, movement). Draining cognitive energy on low-value activies that erode willpower.

**How to get more from it:** Consistent highly quality and adequate sleep as non-negotiable. View movement as foundational as eating and brushing your teeth.

### Money

Money is a tool – a medium of exchange for goods, services, and experiences. Of the core constraints, it is the most malleable: it can be earned, saved, and grown. That makes it a lever for the other two. Managed well, money buys back time, preserves energy, and improves your quality of life.

The most direct application: money can be exchanged for time and energy – outsourcing tasks, automating workflows, hiring help.

How you spend your money is a reflection of what you value, whether the decision is informed or not. **You vote with your dollars.** Every purchase is a small endorsement of the supply chain, the business practices, and the community impact behind it. Price is one metric – it is not the only one. A responsible consumer understands what they are actually supporting when they spend, and factors that into the decision. Recognize that your dollars are doing something in the world whether you're paying attention or not.

**What wastes it:** Spending on things that don't reflect your actual priorities. Failing to track it, which lets small decisions accumulate into a pattern you didn't choose. Underusing it as a lever – not spending where it would genuinely improve your time or quality of life.

**How to get more from it:** Budget with intention. Look for places where money can purchase time or energy. Understand how your dollars move through the economy and what they are supporting.

## Questions for the reader

- Are you satisfied with how you spend your time, energy, and money?
- If not, what would it look like to spend them differently?
