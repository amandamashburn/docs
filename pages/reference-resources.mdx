# Your resources

Every decision you make is made within a set of constraints. You have a finite amount of time, money, cognitive energy, and physical energy. This is the budget you work with every single day.

Most have internalized the idea of budgeting money. Many have started applying the same thinking to time. Fewer have extended that discipline to energy (mental and physical) which deserves equal attention.

The goal: get these resources on your radar so you begin to consistently account for them. Once you do, decision-making improves. You stop treating your time like it's free and your attention like it's infinite. You begin asking the right questions before you take action.

## The three constraints

### Time

Time is your most valuable resource, and it is the only one that cannot be replenished, earned back, or accumulated. An hour spent is gone. There is no refund. You also don't know exactly how much of it you have – you may be granted 18 years or 100.

Most already know this in the abstract. But, few actually budget their time with discipline. The default is drift: saying yes to things that don't matter, letting low-value activities fill high-value hours, and arriving at the end of the week with a vague sense of having been busy but not necessarily spending the time well.

**What wastes it:** Saying yes out of obligation. Context-switching between tasks. Spending high-energy hours on low-stakes decisions. Filling available time with activity rather than asking whether the activity is worth doing.

**How to get more from it:** Do less, better. Be selective – not passive – about where your time goes. Focus on a few areas instead of spreading yourself thin. Build routines that eliminate low-stakes decisions so your time isn't spent managing the structure of your day.

#### I. Cognitive energy

Cognitive energy is the mental fuel that powers all effortful thought – attention, reasoning, decision-making, learning, and self-control. It is finite. It degrades with use. And it does not accumulate: whatever cognitive energy you don't use today doesn't carry over. If you coast through Tuesday, you don't show up Wednesday with a surplus.

The brain defaults toward low-effort processing – habit, pattern recognition, intuition – partly as conservation. When you override those defaults with deliberate thought, the resource drains faster. This is why hard decisions feel cumbersome at the end of a long day. It's why a dense chapter of a difficult book can leave you more tired than a long walk.

**Attention is a useful sub-concept here.** If cognitive energy is how much mental fuel you have, attention is where you direct it. You can have a full tank and still waste it by splitting your focus across ten things instead of concentrating it on one. Or waste it by spending it on activites that are the equivalent of junk food for the mind.

**What wastes it:** Decision fatigue – too many small decisions accumulating before you get to the important ones. A chaotic or friction-heavy environment that demands constant problem-solving. Poor sleep, dehydration, or skipped meals. Passive distraction disguised as rest (endless scrolling is not recovery).

**How to get more from it:** Protect your high-energy hours for your most demanding work. Design your environment to reduce friction rather than constantly fighting it. Build routines that put low-stakes decisions on autopilot. Prioritize sleep, movement, and the basics – these are not optional inputs.

#### II. Physical energy

Physical energy is the body's output capacity. It is most visibly relevant to people whose work or training demands it – athletes, tradespeople, military, etc. But it's not irrelevant to those of us with less physically demanding lifestyles.

Physical condition shapes cognitive performance. Sedentary days accumulate fatigue. Life without consistent movement degrades focus. If you treat your body like an afterthought, it becomes one – and it takes your mental performance with it. You need to take full advantage of your bodies physical capabilities because your physical health is tied to your mental capacity. To neglect your physical capacity and let it go to waste is to also let your cognitive energy be less than it could be and thus wasting potential.

Our budgets for physcical activity are the ones that get wasted most. 

But also, if we waste our days on things that drain our energy (a long commute, relationships that drag us down rather than uplift, doomscrolling) this ultimately impacts our will power for physical exertion. Cognitive energy and physical energy are interlated. One can drain or amplify the other. Unfortunaltey, modern Western medicine tends to treat the mind and body as separate entities. They are not. They are two side of the sime coin. The relationship must be recognized. And you want to be using the energy (in its totality) that you have been given.

For most, the goal isn't optimization of physical output. It's maintenance: keeping the machine running well enough that it isn't limiting everything else.

**What wastes it:** Chronic sedentary behavior. Neglecting recovery (sleep, nutrition, movement). Ignoring what your body is telling you until it forces the issue.

**How to get more from it:** Movement is foundational. Sleep as non-negotiable. The goal is to maintain the baseline.

### Money

Money is a tool – a medium of exchange for goods and services. Unlike the other core constraints, money can be earned, grow, and accumulate. Making it a lever to maximize time and energy or invest into quality of life improvements.

The most direct version of this: money can buy time. Outsourcing tasks you don't need to do yourself, automating processes, hiring help – these are exchanges of money for time and energy.

Just as with the other constraints, how you manage and spend your money is a reflection of what you value whether the decision is conscious or not.

**What wastes it:** Spending on things that don't reflect your actual priorities. Failing to account for it, which lets small decisions accumulate into a pattern you didn't choose. Underusing it as a tool – not spending where it would genuinely improve quality of life or free up time.

**How to get more from it:** Budget with intention. Look for places where money can purchase time, energy, or experience. Track it – not to restrict yourself, but to see clearly what your current spending says about what you've chosen to prioritize.

## The bigger picture

These are resources we have agency to manage and spend. They can be maximally leveraged or collectively wasted.

The discipline being proposed: treat these resources as items to be budgeted and spent wisely. Each decision costs something whether it is made intentionally or not. Pair these resources with your core principles, priorities, and idenity and use these inputs to inform your decision around every action (no matter how big or small) you take.
